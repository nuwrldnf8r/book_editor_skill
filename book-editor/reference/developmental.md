# Developmental editing (the big picture)

The developmental pass asks whether the whole thing works, before any sentence
gets polished. Drawn from John McPhee (*Draft No. 4*), George Saunders (*A Swim
in a Pond in the Rain*), Browne & King (*Self-Editing for Fiction Writers*), and
Anne Lamott (*Bird by Bird*). Work here is proposed and discussed, never imposed —
structure is the author's to own.

## First, honour the draft

A rough first draft is supposed to be rough. Lamott's "shitty first drafts" exist
so the writer can get the raw material down without the editor on their shoulder.
Treat a messy draft as exactly what it should be at this stage, not a failure.
The developmental job is to find the real book inside the raw material.

## The questions, in order

### 1. What is this actually about?
Every chapter and the book as a whole needs a spine — a central question, tension,
or movement. State it in one sentence. If you can't, that's the first finding.
Then test every section against it: does this earn its place, or is it here just
because it happened / because the author wrote it?

### 2. Structure (McPhee: structure is everything)
How is the material ordered, and is that the strongest order? Chronological isn't
always best. Look for: a stronger entry point, sections that should be resequenced,
a structure that mirrors the content. McPhee built whole pieces around their
structure first. For memoir especially, the order of telling is a craft choice,
not just the order things happened.

### 3. Scene vs. summary (Browne & King)
The most common structural problem. Important, emotionally load-bearing moments
should be **dramatized in scene** — shown happening, in time, with detail. Less
important connective material can be **summary**. Drafts usually get the
proportion wrong: they summarize the big moment ("That was the worst year of my
life") and over-dramatize the trivial. Flag what to open up into scene and what
to compress.

### 4. Proportion and pacing (Browne & King)
Where does it drag? Where is it so fast the reader can't feel it? Pace is
controlled by proportion — how much space a thing gets. Slow down for what
matters, speed past what doesn't. A scene that takes three pages to do one page
of work needs compressing; a turning point given two lines needs room.

### 5. Cause, escalation, momentum (Saunders)
Read it as a reader: at each section break, do you *want* to continue? Saunders'
test is whether each unit raises a question that the next answers while raising a
new one. A piece that merely accumulates ("and then, and then") rather than
escalates ("and so, but then") loses momentum. Look for rising stakes and genuine
causality between beats.

### 6. Openings and endings
- **Openings:** drafts usually start too early, with warm-up the writer needed but
  the reader doesn't. The real beginning is often a page or two in. Find it.
- **Endings:** look for the strongest true note. Beware the tidy wrap-up that
  ties a bow on the meaning (this overlaps with the humanizer's "reflective-
  summary ending" tell). The best endings resonate rather than explain.

#### Opening diagnostic (run on the first 1-2 paragraphs, every time)
The opening is the highest-stakes real estate in the book, so check it
explicitly rather than hoping you notice:

1. **Scene or exposition?** Does it open *in a moment* — a person, a place, an
   action — or does it open by generalizing *about* the subject? An opening that
   addresses an abstract "you," makes a universal claim, or lists hypotheticals
   ("A song that... or a moment when... or a piece of music that...") reads as
   essay, not story. This is the "too essay" problem. Memoir and literary fiction
   almost always open stronger in scene.
2. **Is the thesis stated before it's earned?** If the opening tells the reader
   what the book means before any scene has made them feel it, it pre-empts the
   work the book should do. Let the story carry the thesis; let the thesis explain
   the story only later.
3. **Where is the real beginning?** Read down until the writing first comes alive
   in concrete detail. That sentence is often the true opening, and everything
   above it is throat-clearing the writer needed but the reader doesn't.

When the opening is expository, offer the author the options rather than deciding:
cut to the first scene; demote the framing to *after* a concrete moment; or
rewrite the abstraction as a specific, personal image. This is a judgment call —
surface it, don't silently rewrite the opening.

#### Ending diagnostic (run on the last 1-2 paragraphs)
The end of a chapter or prologue should leave the reader leaning forward, not feel
like the writing simply stopped. Check:

1. **Threshold, not summary.** Does it end on a forward pull, or does it close on a
   mission statement / recap ("This book is about X, Y, and Z")? Summaries shut the
   door; thresholds open it. If the last paragraph looks backward at what was
   covered, the real closing line is often an earlier, more forward-facing sentence
   that's been buried beneath the recap — move it last.
2. **Turn toward the reader (the invitation).** The strongest "read on" pull is
   usually *implicit*: end facing the reader or facing forward, and the lean does
   the work. **Avoid the promotional-invitation cliché** ("let's take this journey
   together," "I invite you to join me") — that's an AI/back-cover tell. An earned
   invitation is concrete and quiet.
3. **Earned, not explained.** Beware the tidy wrap-up that ties a bow on the
   meaning (the humanizer's "reflective-summary ending"). Trust the scene.
4. **Don't spend the payoff early.** If the book's biggest claim lands hardest at
   the very end, an earlier chapter shouldn't fire it at full volume. Mind the arc.

### 7. For memoir / narrative non-fiction specifically
- **Emotional throughline:** what is the reader meant to feel moving through this,
  and is it consistent and earned?
- **Honesty:** is the author protecting themselves in a way that flattens the
  story? The most alive memoir usually sits with discomfort rather than resolving
  it. (Don't push the author past what they're willing to share — raise it as a
  question.)
- **Reflection vs. experience:** balance lived scene with the narrator's
  retrospective understanding. Too much reflection turns memoir into essay.

### 8. For fiction specifically
- **Character desire and change:** does the protagonist want something, and are
  they different by the end?
- **Point of view:** is it consistent, and is it the right POV for this story?
- **Stakes and conflict:** is there enough at risk to pull the reader through?

## How to deliver developmental feedback

Write a short **editorial note** in prose, not a checklist:
1. Open with what's genuinely working (specific, not flattery).
2. Name the 2–3 highest-leverage moves — the changes that would help most.
3. Ask the questions only the author can answer (intent, what's true, what they
   want the reader to feel).
4. Propose a sequence and stop for agreement before making structural changes.

Never silently restructure a manuscript. Big moves are the author's call.
