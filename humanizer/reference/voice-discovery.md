# Voice discovery (for new authors)

Use this when the author doesn't yet have a settled voice. The goal is not to
hand them a voice — it's to find the one they already have when they're not
performing "being a writer." Most new authors' real voice shows up when they
talk, and disappears the moment they sit down to write "properly." Your job is
to catch the spoken voice and protect it on the page.

## The interview

Ask a few of these (in chat or via AskUserQuestion). You don't need all of them.

**To surface natural voice:**
- "Tell me this scene the way you'd tell a friend over dinner. Out loud, messy,
  no editing." Then transcribe how they actually phrase it. That phrasing is the
  target.
- "What's the funniest or strangest true detail in this chapter?" New writers
  often cut their best, weirdest, most human material because it feels
  unserious. It's usually the most alive thing they have.
- "Read me the one sentence in your draft that feels most like *you*. Now the
  one that feels most fake." The gap between them is the voice problem in
  miniature.

**To calibrate (targets, not templates):**
- "Name two or three books whose voice you'd love yours to feel like." Use these
  to triangulate register and rhythm. Never copy them.
- "Do you want the reader to feel like you're talking to them, or like they're
  overhearing you think?" (Direct address vs. interiority — a big voice fork.)

**To set guardrails:**
- "What's a word or phrase that would make you cringe if it showed up in your
  book?" (Catches therapy-speak, corporate words, whatever they personally
  reject.)
- "Sentimental or dry? When in doubt, which way should I lean?"

## Draft a voice profile, then confirm it

Turn the answers into 4–6 concrete lines and read it back for a yes/no. Example:

> Your voice, as I read it:
> - Short, plain sentences with the occasional long one when you're on a roll.
> - Concrete over abstract. You'd rather show the burnt toast than name the
>   feeling.
> - Dry humor; allergic to sentimentality.
> - Comfortable sitting in discomfort without resolving it.
> - "Mom" and "Dad," never "my mother" and "my father."
> Sound right?

Adjust until they say yes. Keep the confirmed profile in front of you for the
whole edit. Every sentence you rewrite should be answerable to it.

## A note on developing voice over time

Voice firms up by writing, not by being told what it is. As you edit more of
their pages, update the profile. Point out, specifically, the moments where they
sounded most like themselves: "This paragraph — the bit about the butter knife —
that's your voice. Do more of that." Positive, specific reinforcement teaches
voice faster than a list of don'ts.
